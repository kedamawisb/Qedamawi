Sonder

We, human beings, are all guilty of feeling like we are the chosen ones. You, somehow, out of more than eight billion people on Earth, feel like you are different; more aware, more conscious, more tuned in to the world than those around you. Whether you believe God granted you a deeper understanding of the universe, or simply that your mind works on a level others can't reach, this feeling is familiar to almost everyone. It is the quiet conviction that you see what others miss.

This sense of heightened awareness is common enough to have a name. Introspection, or deep thinking, is the mental state of analyzing information carefully, pondering details, and connecting them into a broader understanding of the world. Most people believe they possess this quality naturally. But the feeling of being a deep thinker and actually being one are two very different things. True awareness demands honest, rigorous scrutiny of both the world and thyself; which poses the question, "Are we really honest to ourselves?".

The "I will start tomorrow", "I know what I am doing", "I am smarter than the average", "It's not my fault" etc. All these lies and self-deception that you and I tell ourselves on a daily basis are pathetic and delusional. Yet by degrees these falsehoods weave themselves into our very souls, till truth's light is but a distant shadow we dare not face. A lie told enough times becomes the truth. And quite regrettably, very few people use it to hack their brains that way to do something productive e.g. Richard Feynman's way of educating, Mother Theresa's and Pope Shenouda's way to loving or Edmund Husserl's concept of epoché. It commonly is the "I've tried my best" and "I did all I could", the "I am already great and I don't need to prove that", and many countless ineffectual lies flooded into our brains to make us feel comfortable about a situation.

Mathematically, the harsh truth is that roughly 95% of the world's population falls between an IQ of 70 and 130. That leaves a razor thin slice at the top for the truly exceptional, a mere 0.1% qualifying as what we would genuinely call a genius. Yet somehow, the rooms we walk into seem filled with people utterly convinced they are among that 0.1%. Making the quiet belief that you are one of them either a rare statistical truth – or the most common delusion known to man.

It is not my slightest intention to fill you in with the motivation to show what you're capable of. But to introduce you to the concept of sonder. Sonder is the profound realization that every random passerby, every social media commentor, and quite literally everyone you see or communicate with is living a life as full, complex and vivid - complete with personal ambitions, struggles and grief. The term originated by author and neologist John Koenig for his project that creates new words for emotions lacking names in 2012, The Dictionary of Obscure Sorrows.

This is where the "I am better than the average" fades. Since you can't be better than the average if everybody else is above average. You can be better or worse than somebody you know nothing about. If you truly understand and experience the feeling of sonder, it will have you settle for average, right? Ironically, it does the opposite. The wise and thorough sense of sonder can be felt anytime and anywhere, it hits unexpectedly. Truly feeling it makes you think, "Wait, this person is running a marathon while having to take care of her children? What am I doing?". It raises honesty and self-awareness.

The best way to tackle this very obstacle from blocking your path from where you are to where you want to be is to actively feel sonder. By practicing mindful awareness, perspective taking and cultivating empathy, we pragmatically live. Where honesty towards thyself becomes nothing but inevitable. Life becomes more engaging, absorbing, fascinating and intriguing, since you undoubtedly will be confronted by your consciousness and challenged to encounter the problems you face logically. And not the "I want to be disgustingly well-educated" goal social media algorithms feed you.

But since we're in a world that comforts you regardless of how irrelevant you become, the self-deception becomes incremental. It was acceptable to point out the truth in the 1960s when John F. Kennedy openly declared "There's nothing more unfortunate than raising fat and chubby children". Subsequently, he enforced fitness standard in high schools. He blatantly expressed aversion to obesity. However, in 2025, a term that defends the unavoidable slothfulness that comes with gaining weight guilt-free is commonly used, "Fatphobia". The term can be easily manipulated among individuals due to its vague combination of the words "Fat" simply meaning naturally oily substance occurring in animal bodies and "Phobia" meaning an extreme and/or irrational fear of or disliking of something. Had we not come up with terminologies that excuse us from literal facts, practicing sonder would become incredibly easy. The terminology mentioned above defends that person from criticism and makes them complacent while villainizing and castigating the 'offender'. Sometimes it is substantially effortless to play the victim.

Conversely, there are overweight individuals that have it immensely harder. Some have conditions like hypothyroidism caused by hormonal imbalances, some might be fighting insulin resistance, and in most cases, genetics account for up to 70% of the variation in body weight. There are more than a handful of reasons why someone might struggle with their BMI. Sonder helps us understand their grapple and toil. It puts us in their shoes where we become less judgmental and seek the truth.

Thus, being able to live truly, towards yourself and others certainly forces you to deep awareness. The deeper your awareness, the more sonder finds you. But here is the truth, you don't have to live honestly as it is always about what you want out of life. Some people love to exist instead of living, some have no values, passions or interests. But only when you are honest will you know what you truly want, and whether you have the courage to pursue it.

For practicing sonder is an art. A few minutes each day you are no longer the main character, no longer the center of the universe, no longer the hero of a story written around you. You and I are simply one of billions, each carrying a terrifyingly different world inside them as vivid and heavy as your own. And somehow, you have to just learn how to, be.
